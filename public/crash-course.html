⭐ VWAP Is Your Anchor

The whole rule: Price above VWAP → only look for buy/long setups. Price below VWAP → only look for sell/short setups. When price is sitting right ON VWAP, wait — the market hasn't decided yet.

Charts & Candlestick Patterns 🕯️
Candlestick patterns are the language price uses to communicate. Day traders read them on 5-minute charts to time exact entries. Swing traders read them on daily charts to confirm reversals and continuations. Master these five patterns and you can read any chart.

How to Read a Candle

🟢 Green / Bullish Candle
📦 Body — From Open to Close. Green = closed ABOVE where it opened.
📏 Upper wick — Price went this high but fell back.
📏 Lower wick — Price went this low but recovered.
🔴 Red / Bearish Candle
📦 Body — From Open to Close. Red = closed BELOW where it opened.
📏 Upper wick — Sellers rejected the high.
📏 Lower wick — Buyers defended the low briefly.
🧩 Think of It Like This

A candle is a tug-of-war between buyers and sellers over a fixed time period. The body shows who won — green means buyers dragged the rope further their way by the end, red means sellers did. The wicks show how far the rope got pulled in each direction before snapping back. A long wick means one side made a big push... and lost.

Chapter 4
Support, Resistance & Volume 📡
Support and resistance are the most important price levels in any chart. Combined with volume analysis, they tell you exactly where to enter, where to set your stop, and where price is most likely to reverse or break through.

Support — The Floor

Support is a price level where buyers consistently step in and stop a decline. The more times price bounces at a level, the stronger it is. When you see a stock pull back to the same price three times and hold — that is a major support level.

How to find it: On the Daily chart, look for places where price reversed direction after touching a specific level on multiple separate occasions. Draw your horizontal line at the wicks (not the bodies) of those reversal candles.

Resistance — The Ceiling

Resistance is a price level where sellers consistently push price back down. Round numbers ($100, $200, $50) are natural resistance because large institutional limit orders cluster there. Previous highs are also strong resistance.

🧩 Think of It Like This

Support is a trampoline. Price falls onto it and bounces back up — the more times it bounces without breaking, the stronger the trampoline. Resistance is a glass ceiling. Price rises and bumps its head — until one day it hits hard enough to shatter the glass and keeps going. Once that ceiling breaks, the broken glass becomes the new floor (the Flip Rule below).

The Flip Rule — Support Becomes Resistance

💡 The Flip Rule

When price breaks ABOVE resistance on strong volume → that old resistance level becomes the new support. When price breaks BELOW support on high volume → that support becomes the new resistance. This is one of the most reliable patterns in all of technical analysis.

ScenarioSignalDay TradeSwing Trade
Bounce at support✅ Buyers hereLong entry — stop below supportSwing long setup
Rejection at resistance⚠️ Sellers hereShort entry or exit longsTake partial profits
Breakout above resistance + volume✅ Strong breakoutMomentum long — enter breakoutAdd to position
Breakdown below support + volume❌ BreakdownShort — stop above old supportExit immediately

Volume — The Truth Detector

Volume tells you whether a price move is real or fake. High volume on a move = institutional participation = reliable signal. Low volume on a move = weak, likely to reverse.

  • Breakout on high volume: Valid, likely to continue. Trade it.
  • Breakout on low volume: Fake breakout — often snaps back. Wait for volume confirmation.
  • Reversal on high volume: Major buyers/sellers entering. Respect the signal.
  • Average Daily Volume: Know each stock's average. A move on 3× average volume is significant. A move on 0.5× average is weak.
✅ The Four-Factor Confirmation

Highest-probability setups have all four: (1) At support or resistance, (2) Confirming candlestick pattern (Hammer / Engulfing), (3) Volume above average, (4) Indicator confirmation (RSI oversold at support, or RSI overbought at resistance). All four = trade. One or two = wait.

🔥 Day Trader — Volume Spikes

Volume spikes on 1-minute and 5-minute charts signal institutional orders entering. When you see a 3–5× volume spike on a candle near a key level, that is a major player taking a position. The move that follows is usually directional and fast. Never fight high-volume moves.

🌊 Swing Trader — Volume and 50 SMA

For swing trades, the 50-day SMA is one of the most powerful institutional support/resistance levels. Large funds buy pullbacks to the 50 SMA in uptrending stocks. When price touches the 50 SMA + forms a Hammer + volume is above average — that is your cleanest swing entry setup.

Key Indicators — EMA, VWAP, RSI, MACD 📡
Indicators do not predict the future. They help you confirm what price is already telling you. Using one indicator alone is weak. Using three indicators that all agree on the same signal is powerful. Here are the four indicators every day and swing trader needs.

1 — EMA: Exponential Moving Average

An EMA smooths price data to show the direction of the trend. It reacts faster to recent price changes than a simple moving average.

EMABest ForHow to Use It
9 EMADay trading momentumShort-term trend direction. Price above 9 EMA = bullish bias. Below = bearish. 9 EMA pullback bounces = day trade entries.
20 EMASwing trade pullbacksIn uptrend: price pulling back to 20 EMA = swing buy signal. 20 EMA above 50 SMA = healthy uptrend.
9 EMA crossing 20 EMATrend change9 EMA crosses above 20 EMA = bullish momentum shift. Below = bearish shift. Use as confirmation, not trigger alone.

2 — VWAP: Volume Weighted Average Price

🧩 Think of It Like This

Imagine everyone who bought gas today wrote down the price they paid. VWAP is the average of all those prices — but weighted so that the times with the most cars (volume) count more. If gas is currently selling for less than that average, it "feels cheap" relative to today — buyers notice. If it's selling for more, it "feels expensive" — sellers notice. That's exactly why VWAP acts as a magnet and a measuring stick all day.

VWAP is the average price of every trade that happened today, weighted by volume. It is the institutional benchmark — large funds measure their execution against VWAP. This is why it acts as one of the strongest intraday support/resistance levels.

🧩 Why This Actually Works

Large institutions often instruct their execution algorithms to buy or sell a position "at or better than VWAP" over the course of the day — it's literally written into their orders. That means there is real, continuous buying pressure near VWAP from below and real selling pressure from above, all day, from accounts far larger than any retail trader. You're not following a magic line — you're following where the biggest players in the market are mechanically required to transact.

  • Price above VWAP = buyers in control. Lean long. Best trades are calls, longs, and buy setups.
  • Price below VWAP = sellers in control. Lean short. Best trades are puts, shorts, and sell setups.
  • VWAP Reclaim: Price dips below VWAP then pushes back above = powerful bullish signal. Institutions defended VWAP.
  • VWAP Rejection: Price bounces at VWAP from below and fails = bearish signal. Stay short.
  • VWAP resets every day at open. It is only used on intraday charts (1-min, 5-min, 15-min).

3 — RSI: Relative Strength Index

RSI measures the speed and magnitude of recent price moves on a 0–100 scale. It tells you if a stock has moved too far too fast and may be due for a reversal.

Bullish divergence: Price makes a lower low but RSI makes a higher low = momentum is improving even as price falls. Often precedes reversals upward. Bearish divergence: Price makes a higher high but RSI makes a lower high = momentum fading at the top. Often precedes reversals downward. These are some of the best signals in technical analysis.

4 — MACD: Moving Average Convergence Divergence

MACD shows the relationship between two EMAs (12 and 26) to reveal momentum and trend changes. It has three components: the MACD line, Signal line, and Histogram.

  • MACD crosses ABOVE Signal line = bullish crossover → momentum shifting up. Enter or add to longs.
  • MACD crosses BELOW Signal line = bearish crossover → momentum shifting down. Exit or tighten stops.
  • Histogram growing positive = bullish momentum increasing. Hold longs.
  • Histogram shrinking from positive = momentum fading. Consider reducing position.
  • MACD is best on Daily chart for swing traders and 5-minute chart for day traders' trend confirmation.
✅ How to Use All Four Together

You do not need all four on every trade — but the more that agree, the stronger the signal. A swing trade entry checklist: Price at support ✓ + Hammer candle ✓ + RSI below 40 and rising ✓ + MACD bullish crossover ✓ = four confirmations. That is a high-conviction trade. One signal = wait. Four = act.

⚠️ A Note on Slippage and Spreads

The math examples in this course use clean numbers — entry at exactly $54.40, stop at exactly $54.10. In real trading, fills aren't always perfect. On a fast-moving stock, your stop might fill a few cents past your trigger price (called slippage), and on lower-volume stocks the gap between the bid and ask price (the spread) eats into your numbers on both entry and exit. Build in a small buffer: if your plan is "exactly 2:1," real-world results will often land closer to 1.7:1 or 1.8:1 after these costs. Stick to liquid, higher-volume stocks (the ones on your watchlist from Chapter 1) to keep this effect small.

  • Partial exit: At 50% of target, sell half. Move stop to breakeven on the rest. Free money trade.
  • Trailing stop: As price moves in your favor, drag your stop up (for longs). Lock in gains as it runs.
  • Time stop: If you are in a trade past 11:30 AM and it hasn't moved, exit. You don't need to hold until noon slop.

Position Sizing Formula

🧮 How Many Shares to Buy

Shares = (Account × Risk %) ÷ (Entry Price − Stop Price)

Example: $10,000 account, 2% risk, entry at $80, stop at $77

🧠 Trading Psychology — The Real Risk

Every rule in this chapter is easy to understand. Almost nobody follows them perfectly — not because the rules are hard, but because of what happens in your head in the moment. Psychology is where most traders actually lose money, not strategy.

The TrapWhat It Looks LikeThe Fix
FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)Jumping into a trade late because "it's already moving and I don't want to miss it"If you missed the entry, you missed the trade. There is always another setup. Chasing turns a good system into a bad one.
Revenge TradingTaking a bigger, sloppier trade right after a loss to "win it back"This is covered by your 2-loss daily limit. The rule exists specifically for this moment — follow it even when it feels wrong.
Moving Your StopPrice approaches your stop, so you move it further away "to give it room"A stop you can move isn't a stop. If you wouldn't enter the trade with that wider stop fresh, don't widen it now.
Overconfidence After WinsA few winners in a row, so you size up dramatically or skip your checklistEvery trade gets the same process — entry, stop, target, size — regardless of your last result. Consistency beats streaks.
Analysis ParalysisAdding more and more indicators because you don't "feel" confident enough to pull the triggerIf support/resistance + VWAP + a candle pattern all agree, that's enough. More indicators rarely add more certainty — they add more noise.
✅ The One-Sentence Fix

Decide your entry, stop, target, and size before you're in the trade — and then your only job once you're in it is to follow the plan you already made. Every psychological trap above happens when a decision gets made mid-trade instead of before it.

🧾 Taxes — What to Know

This isn't tax advice — talk to a licensed tax professional for your specific situation. But here's the basic shape of it so nothing surprises you:

  • Day trades and short-term swing trades (held under 1 year) are taxed as ordinary income — the same rate as your salary, not the lower "long-term capital gains" rate.
  • Every closed trade is a taxable event — profit or loss. Your broker will send you a 1099 form summarizing the year, but you (or your accountant) need to actually report it.
  • Losses can offset gains — if you have winning and losing trades in the same year, losses reduce your taxable profit. Keep records of everything.
  • Set money aside as you go — many new traders spend their gains and get a tax bill they didn't plan for. A simple habit: every time you withdraw profit, set aside 20–30% for taxes in a separate account.

Alex closes the laptop for a while. The midday chop isn't worth fighting — that lesson from Chapter 6 saved a lot of "give-back" trades in the past. Time for lunch, a walk, normal life.

Trade log gets updated: ticker, entry, exit, stop, position size, result, and one note — "Textbook ORB. Resisted FOMO on the unrelated stock. No changes needed."
