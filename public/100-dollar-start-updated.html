Chapter 6

Think in Percentages, Not Dollars

Most traders starting out evaluate trades in dollar amounts. "I made $10 today." "I lost $7." The problem is that $10 means completely different things on a $100 account (+10%) and a $10,000 account (+0.1%). The number alone tells you almost nothing about whether your trading is working.

The traders who last the longest all think this way — not in dollars, in percentages. Once that shift clicks, your performance becomes comparable across any account size, and your risk scales automatically as you grow.

R-Multiple — Your Real Report Card

"R" simply means "how much you risked." If you risked $1 and made $3, that's +3R. If you risked $1 and lost $1, that's -1R. Your R-multiple measures results against the risk taken — not just the dollar amount.

ResultIn R TermsOn $50 account (1% risk = $0.50)On $500 account (1% risk = $5.00)
Great win+3R+$1.50+$15.00
Normal win+1R+$0.50+$5.00
Breakeven0R$0$0
Planned loss-1R-$0.50-$5.00

The same performance (+3R, +1R, -1R) produces completely different dollar results depending on account size — but the skill level required is identical. Build the skill at $50. It transfers directly to $5,000.

🌍 Real Life — Think of It Like This

Think about two people who both lost $50 last week. One has a $500 account. One has a $50,000 account. Same dollar amount — completely different situations. The first person lost 10% of everything they had. The second lost 0.1% and barely noticed. That's why percentages matter more than dollars. Once you start thinking this way, your account size stops defining how you feel about a trade.

⬡ Position size only matters if you're entering at the right location. A $20 position bought in "premium" (top of the range) has worse risk than a $100 position bought in "discount" (bottom of the range). With a small account, location-based entries are your greatest edge — you can't afford to size big, so you need to enter where the probability is already in your favor.
Chapter 7

Position Sizing — The One Formula You Need

Position sizing is the answer to: "How much do I actually buy or sell?" It's a calculation, not a feeling. The formula is the same across every market:

📐 The Formula — Works in Every Market

Step 1: Decide the maximum you're willing to lose on this trade (1–2% of your account).
Step 2: Find your stop-loss distance (how far from entry to your stop, in ticks, pips, or dollars).
Step 3: Divide Step 1 by Step 2 to get your position size.

Example (Forex, $100 account):
Max risk = 1% = $1.00  |  Stop = 10 pips  |  Micro lot pip value = $0.10
$1.00 ÷ ($0.10 × 10 pips) = $1.00 ÷ $1.00 = 1 micro lot ✅

Example (Futures, $100 account):
Max risk = 1% = $1.00  |  Stop = 4 ticks on MES  |  Tick value = $1.25
$1.00 ÷ ($1.25 × 4 ticks) = $1.00 ÷ $5.00 = 0.2 contracts → round down to 0
This tells you the stop is too wide for a $100 account on MES. Tighten the stop to 1 tick ($1.25) or wait until you have more capital.

The formula doing "0 contracts" or "0 lots" is not a failure — it's the math telling you the trade doesn't fit your account right now. That's valuable information. Skip that trade and wait for one that fits.

Chapter 8

How to Actually Grow the Account — Selling, Reinvesting, and Stacking

Most books talk about entries. Very few talk about what comes after a winning trade — which is where the real growth happens. Taking a profit and then just leaving the money sitting is the slow way. The faster way is reinvesting it strategically so each win builds your capacity for the next one.

The Reinvestment Loop

The basic mechanic is simple. You start with $100 and risk 1% per trade — that's $1. You win a trade at 2:1 reward-to-risk — your account is now $102. Next trade, 1% risk = $1.02. Small difference, but it compounds. After 50 consistent trades with a real edge, the position sizes have grown meaningfully — not because you deposited more, but because your wins funded the growth.

📐 The Reinvestment Math

Start: $100 account. 1% risk = $1 per trade. Average win = +2R = +$2.
After 10 winning trades (at 50% win rate, so 20 total trades): ~$120
Now: 1% risk = $1.20 per trade. Same percentage, bigger dollar amount.
After another 20 trades: ~$144
Key point: You didn't add any money. The wins compounded into larger position sizes, which produced larger dollar gains from the same percentage performance.

Multiple Positions — Why $100 Moves Faster Than $50

At $50, you're typically waiting for one trade to close before opening the next. At $100, you can run two positions simultaneously — say, a swing trade on a stock and a day trade on a forex pair. Both are correctly sized at 1% of your account. Neither affects the other. But now you have two chances per session for a win to compound into the account, instead of one.

$1002 — a day trade and a swing trade, or two different marketsTwo income streams running at once. Losses in one can be offset by wins in the other.

When to Take Profits and When to Let It Run

This is the question nobody gives you a straight answer on. Here's one:

1
Take half at your first target

When the trade reaches your planned target, close half. This locks in a guaranteed win regardless of what happens next — and removes all pressure from the remaining half.

2
Move stop to breakeven on the remaining half

Now you can't lose on this trade. The worst outcome is breakeven. Let the second half run until the next resistance level or until your stop gets hit.

3
Reinvest the profit on the next qualifying setup

Not the next trade you see — the next setup that meets all your criteria. That's the difference between compounding and gambling.

🌍 Real Life — Think of It Like This

Think of your trading account like a small business. A food truck doesn't take every dollar it makes on Monday and spend it on supplies for Tuesday. It takes a percentage of the profit, reinvests it into inventory, keeps a reserve for slow days, and gradually expands the menu as the cash flow grows. Your account works the same way. Profit from a trade isn't a reward to spend — it's capital to deploy on the next setup.

Day Trading With a Small Account

Day trading means opening and closing positions within the same session — in before the close, out before the close, no overnight risk. Every position starts and ends the same day.

What Day Trading Actually Requires

What You NeedWhat That Actually Means
Focused time at the screenAt minimum: the hour after the market opens (9:30–11 AM ET for US markets, or the equivalent open for futures and forex sessions)
A clear setup in advanceYou must know what you're looking for before the session starts — not "I'll figure it out as I go"
A stop-loss on every tradeNo exceptions. Day trading can move fast in the wrong direction.
The discipline to close flatHolding a "day trade" overnight because you don't want to take the loss turns it into a very different risk

The 3 Setups That Work at Small Account Size

1. Range Trading — Buy at the Floor, Sell at the Ceiling

When price has bounced between the same two levels at least twice, that range is confirmed. Buy near support (the floor), sell near resistance (the ceiling). Stop just below the floor. Works best during quieter sessions when price isn't trending strongly in one direction.

2. Opening Range Breakout (ORB) — The Morning Setup

Mark the high and low of the first 15–30 minutes after the market opens. When price closes convincingly beyond either level — ideally with increased volume — enter in that direction. Stop goes back inside the range. Most consistent for index futures (MES, MNQ) and major forex pairs during the London/New York overlap.

3. Trend Continuation — Trade With the Wind

If the overall trend is up (price above a rising 20 EMA), only look for long trades. If it's down, only look for shorts. Wait for a pullback to the EMA, see a reversal candle, then enter in the trend direction. Never fight the trend on a small account — you don't have the cushion to be wrong for long.

⚠️ What to Avoid on a Small Day-Trading Account

Trading news events: Spreads widen, fills are unpredictable, and by the time you react, the move is often over.
High leverage: At $100, even 5x leverage means a 20% move wipes you out. Start at 1x–2x.
Overtrading: 3–5 trades a day is plenty. More trades = more commissions = your account shrinks even without losses.
Switching markets constantly: Pick one or two symbols. MES and EUR/USD together is more than enough to start.

🌍 Real Life — Think of It Like This

Day trading is like a street food vendor who sets up in the morning, sells all day, and packs up before dark — no inventory carried home overnight. The range trade is parking at the same corner every day because you know exactly when the lunch crowd arrives. The ORB is that burst of customers the moment you open the window. Trend continuation is following the crowd that's already moving — you didn't start the parade, but you're in it.

Swing Trading With a Small Account

Swing trading means holding a position for more than one day — usually two days to a few weeks. You're capturing a "swing" in price rather than a same-day move.

Why Swing Trading Often Works Better for Small Accounts

The 3 Core Swing Setups

1. Trend Pullback — Clean and Reliable

In a confirmed uptrend (higher highs, higher lows), price periodically pulls back. Buy during pullbacks toward a rising 20 EMA or prior support level — not at the top of a new surge. Stop below the pullback low. Target: prior high. The trend is already doing the work — you're just joining it at a better price.

2. Support Bounce — Simple and Clean

A level that price has bounced from at least twice becomes a high-probability entry on the third touch — especially with a reversal candle (Hammer, Bullish Engulfing). Entry at the candle close. Stop just below the support level. Target: next resistance.

3. Pre-Market Gap + Direction (Futures/Forex)

When index futures or a currency pair gaps significantly overnight, it often continues in that direction in the early session — especially if the gap was driven by a real catalyst (central bank decision, economic data, major news). This is where swing traders who hold overnight can capture moves that day traders never see — because the move happened between sessions while the day trader had no position.

💡 The Overnight Opportunity

This is something the day trading section cannot offer: a swing trader who holds through the night can wake up to find their position already profitable before the market even formally opens. This is called a gap in your favor — and it's one of the most common ways experienced swing traders book their best results. It requires a correctly-sized position and a stop-loss already in place before you go to sleep.

Managing Overnight Risk

That overnight opportunity cuts both ways. News that breaks after the close can push price right through your stop-loss while you're asleep — and your actual exit ends up worse than what you planned. It happens. Here's how to keep it from becoming a problem:

1
Size correctly — always

At 1–2% risk, even a gapped-through stop results in a loss of 2–3%. Painful, but your account survives.

2
Check the economic calendar before opening a swing trade

If a major central bank decision or jobs report drops tomorrow, factor that into timing your entry.

3
Reduce size before weekends

Forex can gap on Sunday open when geopolitical news hits. Consider trimming or closing Friday afternoon.

🌍 Real Life — Think of It Like This

A swing trader is like a surfer who paddles out and waits for the right wave — not the first one, not every ripple, but the wave actually worth riding. Once on it, they ride it as long as it's going in the right direction, then get off cleanly before it crashes. Day traders are sprinters. Swing traders are distance runners. Neither is better — they just require different gear, different preparation, and different patience.

The Compounding Map — From $100 Forward

This is what consistent, realistic trading actually produces over time. Not "turn $100 into $1 million." Real percentages, real win rates, real timelines — and the two mechanics that actually make the account grow faster.

The Two Things That Actually Accelerate Growth

1. Sell, Take the Profit, and Reinvest

This sounds obvious but most people don't actually do it consistently. The compounding math only works if you take profits out of trades and put them back into the next one at the correct size. A $100 account that wins $10 and then trades the same $1 position forever is not compounding — it's flat. A $100 account that wins $10 and now trades $1.10 positions (1% of $110) is compounding. Small difference at first. Massive difference at 50 or 100 trades.

The habit: after every winning trade, recalculate your 1% risk based on the new account balance before placing the next trade. That's it. That simple step is the difference between linear results and exponential ones.

2. Multiple Positions Speed Things Up

At $100 you have enough to run 2–3 simultaneous positions at 1% risk each — without ever breaking the risk rule. A day trader with $100 can have one MES trade open AND one forex position going at the same time, each risking $1. Two positions means two chances to win in the same session. It also means two chances to lose — which is why the 1% rule per position is non-negotiable when you're running multiples.

Swing traders can stack this even further. Three stocks in different sectors held for a week, each at $1 risk — a winning week on all three produces more than any single trade could. A losing week on all three still only costs $3. The math stays controlled; the opportunity multiplies.

💡 The Rule When Running Multiple Positions

Each position gets its own 1–2% risk budget. If you have $100 and run three trades at 1% each, you're risking $3 total — 3% of the account on any given day. That's acceptable. If you run three trades at 3% each, you're risking 9% in a single session. That's not. The per-position rule is the same regardless of how many positions you have open.

The Assumptions

Win rate: 45% (realistic for a disciplined trader starting out — you don't need to win more than half your trades if you follow the next section). Average win: +1.5R. Average loss: -1R. This produces a positive expected value of +0.125R per trade — a real mathematical edge over time.

Most traders starting out don't hit these numbers in the first six months. Not because the strategy doesn't work — because psychology takes time to build. One revenge trade can erase two weeks of disciplined gains. Part 6 addresses exactly this.

🌍 Real Life — Think of It Like This

Compounding a small trading account is like rolling a snowball down a hill. At the top it barely seems worth it — each new layer of snow adds almost nothing visible. But as it rolls, each layer sticks to a bigger surface. By the bottom, the same small amount of snow that seemed negligible at the top is now a huge addition. The only way the snowball stops growing is if you drop it midway — every revenge trade, every skipped stop, every oversized position is dropping the snowball and starting over.

The Rules That Keep You in the Game

⬡ Paper Trading & Journaling — Before You Use Real Money

Do not trade real money until you have 30 days of paper trading results. Paper trading is not "pretend" — it is how you build the pattern recognition and emotional habits you need before real money is on the line.

📝 How to Paper Trade
1. Use your platform's paper/demo account
2. Apply every rule in this course exactly as written
3. Screenshot every entry and exit
4. Record in a journal: date, setup, entry, stop, target, outcome, what you did right/wrong
📓 The Trade Journal — Minimum Info
Per trade, record:
• Date & time
• Ticker & direction
• Why you entered (the setup)
• Where your stop was
• Where your target was
• Result in R-multiples
• One honest note on execution
The 30-Day Test: After 30 days of paper trading, calculate your win rate and average R-multiple. If your expectancy is positive (win rate × avg win − loss rate × avg loss > 0), you have a real edge and can consider going live with small size. If not, keep paper trading and find what is not working.

These six rules aren't suggestions. They're the difference between traders who last long enough for the skill to develop and traders who blow up a small account in their first month and quit. They apply at every account size — and matter even more when the account is small and every trade counts more.

1
Never risk more than 1–2% of your account on a single trade

At $100, 2% = $2. That's your hard limit per trade — not per session, per trade. If a setup doesn't fit within this limit, skip it. At 2% risk, you can lose 10 trades in a row and still have 80% of your account. At 10% risk, 10 losses ends the game.

2
Place your stop-loss the moment you enter

Not when you "get a chance." Not after you see how it goes. The moment your entry fills, the stop order goes in. This is the only protection that works while you're asleep, distracted, or wrong.

3
Never move your stop further away

Widening a stop to avoid taking a loss is how small, planned losses become account-ending ones. Your stop marks the exact price where your trade thesis is proven wrong. Moving it means you're now hoping instead of trading.

4
Wait 30 minutes after a loss before placing another trade

No exceptions. Almost every revenge trade in history was placed within 10 minutes of the loss it was trying to recover. The 30-minute break resets the emotional state that drives that decision.

5
No trade without a full plan

Entry, stop, target, size — all four, before you click. If you can't answer all four, you don't have a trade. You have a guess. The market treats both identically.

6
Log every trade — win or loss

The patterns that cost you money are invisible until you track them. Logging every trade turns random results into data you can actually learn from. This is how you improve faster than everyone who just trades and hopes.

After a Win

The most dangerous moment in early trading is often right after a first win. The brain says "I've got this" and immediately wants to size up, take more trades, or skip the plan. Don't. Keep the same size for at least 20 more trades. One win is a sample size of one. Log it, review it, and continue exactly as before.

After a Loss

There are two kinds of losses. A planned loss — you followed your rules, the stop was placed correctly, price hit it — is the cost of doing business. These are fine. A mistake loss — you moved your stop, skipped your stop, or entered without a plan — is something to learn from specifically. Name the exact thing that broke down. One thing, not "I'm bad at this." Fix that one thing. Continue.

🌍 Real Life — Think of It Like This

These rules are like the safety procedures on an airplane. They seem unnecessary on 99% of flights where nothing goes wrong. But they exist for the 1% where they matter enormously. No one argues with the seatbelt rule during turbulence. Trading rules work the same way — they feel most unnecessary on the good days, and matter most on the days that would otherwise destroy your account.

Full Glossary — Every Term Easy to Follow

Ask Price
The price sellers want. You BUY at the ask. Always slightly higher than the bid.
ATR (Average True Range)
Measures how much a security moves on an average day. Used in screeners to find volatile stocks. ATR% above 5% = meaningful daily movement.
Bearish
Expecting price to go down. Other words for the same idea: short, put option, "to the downside."
Bid Price
The price buyers offer. You SELL at the bid. Always slightly lower than the ask. The gap between bid and ask is the spread.
Breakout
When price closes beyond a support or resistance level it had previously bounced from. Often signals the start of a new move in that direction.
Bullish
Expecting price to go up. Other words: long, call option, "to the upside."
Call Option
A contract giving the right to buy at a set price. Gains value when the underlying price rises. Going "long via options." Bullish bet.
Candlestick
A bar on a price chart showing the open, high, low, and close for one time period. Green = closed higher than open. Red = closed lower than open. The body is the thick part; the wicks are the thin lines above and below.
Day Trading
Opening and closing all positions within the same trading session. No overnight holds. No overnight risk.
EMA
Exponential Moving Average. A line on a chart showing average price, weighted toward recent candles. Price above a rising EMA = uptrend bias. Price below a falling EMA = downtrend bias.
Forex (FX)
The global market for trading currency pairs — EUR/USD, GBP/USD, USD/JPY, and more. Nearly 24 hours a day on weekdays. Measured in pips. No PDT rule.
Fractional Shares
The ability to buy a portion of one share of stock rather than a whole share. Lets you buy into expensive stocks with as little as $1.
Futures Contract
An agreement to buy or sell a set amount of something (an index, commodity, currency) at a set price on a set future date. Most futures traders close before expiration — they trade the price movement, not the underlying asset. No PDT rule.
Gap
A jump in price between one session's close and the next session's open — caused by news, earnings, or events that happened when the market was closed. Gaps can be in your favor or against you.
Leverage
Using borrowed money to control a position larger than your cash. 10:1 leverage on $100 controls a $1,000 position. Both gains and losses are multiplied at the full position size.
Limit Order
An order that only executes at a specific price or better. You set the price; the market comes to you. Doesn't always fill — but when it does, you get the price you planned.
Long
You bought first. You profit if price rises. The standard direction — buying first, profiting when price goes up. Also: bullish, call option.
Lot (Forex)
Forex position size unit. Standard lot = 100,000 units. Mini lot = 10,000 units. Micro lot = 1,000 units (1 pip ≈ $0.10). Start with micro lots.
MACD
Moving Average Convergence Divergence. A momentum indicator showing when price momentum is building or fading. Two lines crossing signals potential direction shifts.
Margin
The money your broker requires as a deposit to open and hold a leveraged position. Like a security deposit on a rental — not the full price, but what you put up to control the full amount.
Margin Call
Your broker's alert that your account has dropped below the required maintenance margin. You must add funds or the position is closed automatically.
Market Order
An order that executes immediately at whatever price is currently available. Fast, but you don't control the exact price. In volatile or low-volume markets, you can get a worse price than expected (slippage).
MES / MNQ
Micro E-mini S&P 500 (MES) and Micro E-mini Nasdaq-100 (MNQ). Micro futures contracts — 1/10th the size of the standard ES and NQ contracts. MES tick = $1.25. MNQ tick = $0.50. Real practical starting point for futures with $50–$100.
ORB
Opening Range Breakout. A day trading strategy that marks the high and low of the first 15–30 minutes after the open and trades the first clean break beyond either level with volume.
PDT Rule
Pattern Day Trader rule. A US regulation that historically required $25,000 in a margin account to make more than 3 day trades in 5 days. FINRA updated this rule in 2026 — check current requirements with your broker. Does not apply to futures or forex.
Penny Stock
A stock trading under $5 per share. Typically very small companies. High volatility, low liquidity, wide spreads. Can move 50–100% in a day — in either direction. High risk — only with strict stops and a clear plan.
Pip
The smallest standard price movement for a forex pair. For EUR/USD, 1 pip = 0.0001. On a micro lot (1,000 units), 1 pip ≈ $0.10.
Put Option
A contract giving the right to sell at a set price. Gains value when the underlying price falls. Going "short via options." Bearish bet.
R-Multiple
How much you made or lost expressed as a multiple of what you risked. +2R = made twice your risk. -1R = lost exactly your planned risk amount. Universal measure of trading performance.
Resistance
A price level the market has repeatedly struggled to break above. Like a ceiling. When price finally breaks through, the old resistance often becomes new support.
RSI
Relative Strength Index. A 0–100 scale measuring whether price has moved too far, too fast. Above 70 = overbought territory. Below 30 = oversold territory. During strong trends, these levels can persist for a long time — RSI above 70 is not automatically a sell signal.
Short
You sold first. You profit if price falls. You buy back later at the lower price and keep the difference. Also: bearish, put option, "going short."
Small Cap Stock
Stocks with a market capitalization (total company value) between roughly $300 million and $2 billion. More volatile than large companies, more liquid than penny stocks. Often produce large single-day moves on earnings or news.
Spread
The difference between the bid (buy) price and ask (sell) price. The broker's built-in cost. A wider spread means more cost per trade. Compare spreads between brokers before choosing one for a small account.
Stop-Loss
A pre-set order that automatically closes your trade if price moves against you by a set amount. The most important risk management tool in trading. Should be placed immediately when you enter a trade — not after.
Support
A price level the market has repeatedly bounced from when falling. Like a floor. When price breaks through it, the old support often becomes new resistance.
Swap (Forex)
A small daily charge or credit applied to forex positions held past a certain time each day. Based on the interest rate difference between the two currencies in the pair. Can be positive (you earn) or negative (you pay).
Swing Trading
Holding trades for more than one day — typically 2 days to a few weeks. Captures larger "swings" in price. Lower commission cost than day trading. Involves overnight risk.
Tick (Futures)
The smallest price increment for a futures contract. Each tick has a set dollar value. MES = $1.25/tick. ES = $12.50/tick. MNQ = $0.50/tick. NQ = $5.00/tick. Always know your tick value before placing a trade.
Volatility
How much a price moves. High volatility = big moves = more opportunity AND more risk. Measured by ATR, beta, or VIX (for the broader market). The same volatility that creates the opportunities in the high-volatility stocks section is the same thing that can end a trade in seconds.
Volume
How many shares, contracts, or units were traded in a given period. High volume confirms that a price move has real participation behind it. Low volume = questionable moves, poor fills, wide spreads. Always check volume before trading a small stock.
