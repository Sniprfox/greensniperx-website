Overview
You've heard it before — rumors that a crash, or a recession, might be coming. They're always circulating somewhere. But every so often, the research starts lining up with the noise too, and the question shifts from "is this just talk?" to "what do I actually do about it?" This guide centers on one answer in detail — a specific, well-defined method using options — and surrounds it with the history, signals, and defensive habits that make it usable responsibly.

This isn't a cover-all-your-bases textbook. It's a playbook: one core method, explained until you actually understand the mechanics, the platform steps, the real dollar math, and — just as importantly — why it doesn't always work. Everything else in this guide exists to support that method: the historical context that calibrates it, the signals that give it context, and the defensive and psychological habits that keep one method from becoming your whole financial plan. If you came here looking for a survey of ten different crash-hedging strategies, this isn't that guide — and that's intentional: real depth on one method beats a shallow pass over many.

A note on examples. Wherever this guide uses a specific ticker (QQQ, for instance) or a specific dollar figure, it's there to make the mechanics concrete — not as a recommendation. The math and the steps work the same on any optionable stock or ETF. You choose what you actually trade.

⚠️ Critical Warning — Read Before Trading Options

Options can go to zero. That is not a figure of speech.

Unlike stocks, which can recover from a loss, an option that expires out of the money is worth exactly $0.00. You can be right about the direction and still lose 100% of your premium if your timing is off. This is not a beginner product. Use this playbook only with money you can afford to lose entirely.

🕐 Theta Decay — The Invisible Enemy
Every day that passes, your option loses value even if the stock doesn't move. This is called theta decay. A 30-day option loses value fast. A 90-day option decays slowly. Never buy short-dated options for crash protection — the market can stay irrational longer than your option has time.
💥 IV Crush — The Post-Event Trap
Before a major event (Fed meeting, earnings, CPI), implied volatility inflates option prices. After the event — even if your direction is correct — volatility collapses and your option loses value. Buying puts the day before a Fed meeting and getting the direction right can still result in a loss.
✅ The Safe Way to Use This Playbook
1. Start with the smallest position size possible — 1 contract
2. Only buy options with 60+ days to expiration to reduce theta risk
3. Never allocate more than 5% of your total portfolio to crash protection at any time
4. Paper trade this strategy for at least 30 days before using real capital
5. If you don't understand what theta, delta, or IV mean — do not trade options yet

What You're Actually Buying

A put option gives you the right to sell a stock at a specific price (the strike price) before a specific date (the expiration date). If you buy a put with a strike price below the current stock price, it's out of the money (OTM) — it only has value if the stock falls below that strike before expiration.

The further OTM a put is, and the sooner it expires, the cheaper it is — because the odds of the stock actually falling that far, that fast, are low. That's why these are cheap. It's not a discount. It's a reflection of probability.

The appeal: if a sharp decline happens before expiration, a put that cost very little can become worth many times more — because it goes from "probably worthless" to "actually usable" almost overnight. That asymmetry — small, known cost vs. a payout that can be a large multiple of it — is the entire reason this method exists.

⬡ Options Expiration — Understanding the Date on Every Contract

Every put option has an expiration date — the date after which the contract is worthless. Choosing the right expiration is one of the two most important decisions when buying crash protection (the other is strike price).

📅 Short-dated (0–30 days)
✔ Cheaper premium upfront
✔ High gamma — moves fast when right
✘ Theta decay accelerates rapidly
✘ Leaves no time if the crash is delayed
📅 Longer-dated (60–120 days+)
✔ More time for the move to develop
✔ Slower theta decay early on
✔ Can roll forward if timing is off
✘ Higher cost, lower leverage per dollar

SPY 480P 03/21

480 = Strike price — the right to sell at $480
P = Put (you profit if price falls)
03/21 = March 21st expiration
→ The further out the expiration, the more time you're buying. For crash protection, give yourself at least 60 days.

The Math That Changes Everything

Option premiums are often shown as small per-share numbers — $0.05, $0.13, $0.34 — easy to mentally round down to "a nickel." But each contract represents 100 shares, so a $0.05 premium actually costs $5 to buy one contract. That's where a "$5 trade" comes from — a nickel per share, times 100.

Scale that to "100 of the same $5 trades," and the total is $500 — not $5, and not nothing. The question isn't "can I afford $5, repeated a bunch of times?" It's "am I comfortable with $500 being the kind of cost that adds up if I do this regularly?" Scaling a position scales the loss exactly as much as it scales the potential gain — that's not a flaw, it's just what leverage means.

A Recent Example: Early 2025

This isn't abstract. In mid-February 2025, the S&P 500 hit a record high. Over the following seven weeks, as new tariff announcements rattled markets, it fell by roughly 19%. The sharpest part came on two consecutive days in early April: the S&P 500 fell roughly 5% one day and 6% the next — two of its worst single days in years. The Nasdaq-100 (QQQ's index) fell even further, dropping about 21% from its own record high and crossing into what's typically called "bear market" territory.

That's the kind of move this method is built around — fast, and far enough to turn a cheap deep-OTM put into something worth many times its cost.

Here's the other half, and it's just as important: the recovery was just as fast. Over the following weeks, the S&P 500 gained back roughly 19.6% — one of its best rallies in decades — fully erasing the year's losses by mid-2025. A put that wasn't positioned for that specific six-to-seven-week window, or that wasn't closed out near the low, would have seen its value evaporate just as quickly as it appeared.

Again — not a prediction. This is simply what "fast and far enough" has looked like recently, and how narrow the window was even when it happened.

Two Outcomes: What a Week Can Look Like

Using the $5-per-contract example from above, here's what the two realistic outcomes actually look like.

A possible win: if a sharp move happens this week — the kind from the 2025 example above — that $5 contract could become worth $30, $50, or more. Turning $5 into $50 is a 10x gain on that one contract; scaled to 100 contracts, that's $500 becoming $5,000.

The more common outcome: the contract expires worth less than $5, often close to zero — a loss of $5 on that one contract. By itself, $5 is a small number. But this is the outcome most weeks, not occasionally — which is exactly why the premium was only $5 to begin with. If you're doing this regularly, that $5 isn't a one-time thing, it's closer to a recurring weekly cost — the same "insurance premium" idea from below.

The win scenario raises its own question: what do you do with it? Sell for the 10x, or hold in case the move keeps going? This guide doesn't prescribe an answer — but it insists this gets decided before you're looking at a real number on a real screen, the same way the dollar limit did. Section 06 is where you write it down.

Two things make landing on a "win" week harder to time — particularly for a common entry trigger, waiting until "everyone" is saying a crash is coming:

  • By the time a decline is widely discussed, implied volatility on puts has usually already risen sharply — the same far-OTM put that cost $5 a month ago might now cost $30–50 just to get in. The "cheap" window tends to close right around when the narrative becomes obvious.
  • A real decline still has to happen fast enough, and far enough, before your specific expiration date — getting the direction right isn't enough; the timing has to work too.

Three Scenarios: How a Week Can Actually Go

All three of these start from the same place — a deep OTM put bought during calm conditions for $0.05/share ($5/contract). These are illustrative, not real trades — same format as the hypothetical earlier in this section, just walked through three ways it can unfold.

Scenario AThe Win

Setup: The position was opened during a quiet stretch — low VIX, nothing in the news. Cost: $5 for the one contract.

What happens: A few weeks later, a sharp decline (the kind from the 2025 example) pushes the stock down fast — far enough, and soon enough, to land before expiration. The contract is now worth $60: a 12x.

Outcome: Because the exit rule was decided in advance (Section 06), the position is closed for the gain rather than held "to see if it goes higher."

Why it worked: All three conditions from this section lined up at once — the move happened, it happened fast, and it happened inside the window. That's the scenario the whole method is built around — and also why it's the least common of the three.

Scenario BThe Normal Outcome

Setup: Same starting point — $5, opened during calm conditions.

What happens: The stock drifts sideways, or even slightly up, over the following weeks. Nothing dramatic occurs. By expiration, the contract is worth a few cents — essentially nothing.

Outcome: The $5 is gone. Per the plan from Section 06, a similar position might get re-established the following week — same $5, same odds, same setup.

Why this isn't a failure: This is the outcome most weeks — by design. The $5 functioned exactly like an insurance premium: paid, and this time, not needed.

Scenario CThe Timing Trap

Setup: Instead of buying during calm conditions, the position is opened after headlines start saying the market might crash. By now, implied volatility has already risen — the same strike and expiration that would have cost $5 a few weeks ago now costs $35.

What happens: A decline does happen — but only a modest one, because part of the move that everyone was reacting to had already occurred before this entry. The contract rises to $40: barely above the $35 paid.

Outcome: After accounting for the bid/ask spread from earlier in this section, this lands close to breakeven or a small loss — despite being right about the direction.

Why this is the trap: Being right about direction wasn't enough, because the entry price had already absorbed most of the move's value before it started. This is the exact dynamic this section and Section 03 both warn about — and it's the scenario "waiting for confirmation" tends to produce.

A Different Way to Think About It

Instead of trying to time entries around "when it sounds like a crash is coming," some people treat a small, fixed slice of their portfolio as an ongoing insurance premium — buying a modest amount of OTM put exposure on a regular schedule, regardless of sentiment. Money spent on options that expire worthless gets treated the same way you'd treat a home insurance premium in a year nothing burned down: the cost of not being wiped out in the years something does.

Pre-Market Gaps: Why the Open Often Matters Most

The regular stock market session runs 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM Eastern Time, but the news that drives a crash rarely waits for the bell. Earnings surprises, economic data, and geopolitical headlines regularly hit overnight or in the early morning — and index futures (which trade nearly around the clock) start reacting immediately. By the time the pre-market session begins around 4 AM, the futures market may already be down sharply, "pricing in" a move that the regular stock market hasn't opened to confirm yet.

The Catch: Most Options Don't Trade Pre-Market

Here's the part that surprises people holding this kind of position overnight: even though the underlying index or stock is already moving in the pre-market session, the options themselves usually aren't trading yet. Your put's real-world value has already shifted based on where the underlying is sitting — but you typically can't act on it until the regular options session opens at 9:30 AM alongside everything else.

That creates a specific, predictable pattern: the single biggest jump in this position's value often happens between yesterday's close and the opening seconds of the next regular session — while you were asleep, and before you can do anything about it. The first few minutes after 9:30 AM can be chaotic, with wide bid/ask spreads as the options market "catches up" to where the underlying already is. A position that closed at $0.05 the day before can open already worth $0.40, $0.60, or more, depending on how large the overnight move was.

Why Section 06's Exit Rule Exists

On a morning like that, there's no time to think clearly — the position has already moved, the screen looks dramatic, and the spread is wide. This is exactly why Section 06 ("Your Plan") asks you to decide your exit rule in advance, while you're calm. Knowing ahead of time whether you'll sell into that opening volatility or wait a few minutes for the spread to settle isn't a small detail — on the mornings this strategy is built for, it's the whole decision.

Two-Sided Insurance: Hedging in Both Directions

Some traders extend that insurance idea in both directions at once — protecting against any sudden, dramatic move, regardless of which way it goes. Here's a version of that I sometimes use: on a given week, I'll set aside a small amount for what I think of as two-sided insurance — a cheap out-of-the-money put and a cheap out-of-the-money call on the same underlying, each typically in the $3–7 per-contract range.

This combination is sometimes called a strangle. The logic: if something unexpected causes a sharp move in either direction — a surprise earnings report, a sudden macro headline, the kind of single-day move from the example above — one side of this pair could become valuable enough to offset losses elsewhere in the account, or simply pay for itself and the other side several times over. If the week passes calmly, both sides likely expire worthless — a small, known cost, the same way the put-only "insurance" position above works.

The same honesty applies here as everywhere else in this guide: most weeks, both sides expire worthless. The point isn't that this makes money on its own — it's that it's a small, fixed cost that's there if something dramatic happens to the rest of your account that week.

The Short-Dated Variant: When Speed Matters More Than Direction

Everything above gets more extreme when expiration is close — say, 5 days out instead of months. Two things happen at once: gamma goes up (the option's price becomes hyper-sensitive to the stock's price — a move that might shift a 60-day option's value by 20% could shift a 5-day option's value by 200%+), and theta goes up just as much (with almost no time left, the option loses value from the simple passage of time very fast, sometimes daily, even if the stock doesn't move).

A hypothetical walkthrough — illustrative numbers only, not a real trade. Say a stock is at $100, and a $90 put expiring in 5 days is priced around $0.50 ($50/contract) — the market's pricing in maybe a 5% chance it gets there.

If the stock drops to $85 within 2 days, that put could realistically jump to $5–8 ($500–800) — a 10–15x move. That's the scenario this approach is built around.

The more common outcome: if the stock instead drifts to $98, or sits flat, that same put might be worth $0.10 by day 4 and essentially nothing by expiration — not because the thesis was "wrong," but because the move didn't happen fast enough. With 5 days, "eventually" doesn't count.

The honest summary: shorter expiration = bigger potential multiple AND a narrower window for it to actually happen. The dollar-math above applies identically — scaling this up scales both sides of that equation.

2008: The Slow Burn With One Violent Week

The 2008 financial crisis is the textbook "slow" crash — the Dow went from its October 2007 high of about 14,165 to a March 2009 low near 6,594, a decline of more than 50% over roughly 17 months. But "slow" doesn't mean "calm" the whole way through. The week of October 6–10, 2008 was the single worst week in the Dow's 112-year history, with the index dropping a record 18% in five trading days — a stretch that included a 7% single-day drop after Congress initially rejected a bank bailout bill.

What happened to individual stocks that week shows just how violent "violent" can get: when central banks announced a coordinated rescue plan the following Monday, Morgan Stanley's stock jumped 87% in a single day — after having been hammered alongside everything else just days before. Same stock, same week-ish, two completely opposite extreme moves.

2020: Five Weeks, Two of Them Brutal

The S&P 500 peaked at 3,386 on February 19, 2020, then fell 34% over the next five weeks to a closing low of 2,237 on March 23. Within that span, at least three individual weeks saw declines of 5% or more — and the weeks of March 13 and March 20 each exceeded 10% on their own.

At the individual-stock level, certain sectors moved far harder than the index. Cruise line stocks Carnival and Norwegian Cruise Line finished 2020 as the two worst performers in the entire S&P 500, each down more than 56%, with most of that damage concentrated in these few weeks. United Airlines ended the year down more than 50%, and when oil demand collapsed, WTI crude futures briefly traded below zero dollars per barrel for the first time ever — an almost unthinkable move that happened within days.

And just as in 2025, the reversal was its own extreme event: the day after the index bottomed, the S&P 500 gained over 9% in a single session — one of its best days ever, arriving immediately on the heels of one of its worst weeks ever.

2025: Seven Weeks, Two Days That Mattered Most

This is the example from Section 01, with one more layer: the individual stock moves on the worst single day. On April 10, 2025 alone, Tesla fell 7.3%, Nvidia dropped nearly 6%, Meta slipped almost 7%, and Apple pulled back 4.2% — a single trading day, not even the worst day of the stretch.

What This Means for Expiration Dates

Look back at the "Worst ~1-Week Stretch" column. In every one of these three crashes, there was a window of just a few trading days where most of the move happened — even in 2008, which took 17 months overall. That's the direct answer to Section 01's expiration question: a 5-day option isn't trying to catch "the crash." It's trying to catch one of these weeks — which history says can show up even inside a slow-moving decline, not just a fast one.

The flip side, also from history: outside of those specific weeks, the same stocks and indexes were comparatively calm — sometimes even rallying hard, as Morgan Stanley did days after its worst week. A short-dated position that isn't sitting through one of these specific windows is sitting through "calm," which is exactly the environment where Section 01 said these options bleed value fastest.

Not a prediction. This section doesn't tell you when the next "worst week" happens, or which stock moves hardest during it. What it shows is that these windows are a real, recurring feature of market history — short, violent, and surrounded by calm — which is the actual thing a 5-day expiration is a bet on.

"What do I do when research and rumors say the market is about to crash?" That question is the reason this section exists — and the honest answer starts with separating those two words.

Two Very Different Things: Research vs. Rumors

Rumors are sentiment — headlines, social media posts, a coworker who "heard from a guy." They spread fast, they're often vague about timing, and by the time something is being widely repeated as a rumor, the market has frequently already started pricing it in.

Research is data with a track record — things like the indicators below, which have been studied across decades of market history. Research doesn't tell you "sell on Tuesday." It tells you things like "the probability of a downturn sometime in the next year is elevated" — useful, but a very different kind of useful than a rumor implies.

The rest of this section covers the research side. Keep it in mind as you read: every one of these tools answers "where are we in the cycle," and none of them answer "what happens Tuesday."

⬡ When a crash break happens, it looks like this. Price auctions within the 68% value zone, then makes a "strong break" — a decisive close outside the range that leaves an imbalance zone behind.

VIX — The "Fear Gauge"

The VIX measures how much movement options traders expect in the S&P 500 over the next 30 days, based on what they're currently paying for options. When VIX is low, options — including the puts from Section 01 — are relatively cheap, because the market expects calm. When VIX spikes, it means the market is pricing in much bigger expected moves, and those same options get noticeably more expensive across the board.

This is the direct link back to Section 01: a low-VIX environment is when deep OTM puts are cheapest to establish. A high-VIX environment is usually when everyone's already talking about how scary things are — and the "cheap" window has typically already closed.

The Yield Curve

This compares interest rates on short-term vs. long-term government bonds. Normally, longer-term bonds pay more. When that flips — short-term rates higher than long-term — it's called an inversion, and it's one of the most closely watched recession signals that exists. A yield curve inversion has preceded every US recession going back to 1971, with only one false alarm in that entire span.

Here's the part that matters most: the typical lead time from an inversion to the start of a recession runs roughly 6 to 18 months, averaging around a year — and equity markets often keep rising for a while after the inversion before eventually peaking, with the S&P 500 in one well-known case gaining about 14% after the 2006 inversion before topping out in October 2007. So an inversion isn't a "sell now" signal — it's a "the clock has started, but nobody knows how long the clock is" signal. Some research suggests the more dangerous moment is actually when an inverted curve starts normalizing again after the first rate cut, not the inversion itself — which is exactly the kind of nuance that gets lost when this shows up as a rumor ("the yield curve inverted, sell everything!").

Economic Calendar

Certain recurring releases — the monthly jobs report, inflation data (CPI), and Federal Reserve meeting announcements — tend to cause outsized moves because they resolve uncertainty all at once. For options specifically, this often shows up as implied volatility rising in the days before the event (making options more expensive) and dropping sharply afterward — a pattern often called "IV crush." Relevant if you're timing anything around scheduled events rather than unexpected ones.

Reading the Market Map: What Tends to Move Together

Before looking at any individual setup, it helps to know which things are basically the same thing wearing different labels, which things tend to move together but with different intensity, and which things move for separate reasons entirely.

The "Same Thing, Three Tickers" Group

SPY, VOO, and IVV all track the S&P 500 — the 500 largest US companies. They move essentially identically to each other. If you're checking "what's the broad market doing," any one of them tells you the same story.

Same Direction, Bigger Swings

QQQ tracks the Nasdaq-100 — a tech-heavy index. It tends to move in the same direction as SPY on most days, but the moves are usually bigger in both directions. A day where SPY is up 0.5% might see QQQ up 0.8–1%; a sharp down day in SPY often sees QQQ down even more. I check QQQ specifically when I want a sense of how "aggressive" today's move is, not just its direction.

The "Market Leaders" Group

Stocks like Microsoft, Apple, and NVIDIA carry enormous weight in these indexes — their individual moves can shift SPY and QQQ themselves, and the broader index's direction tends to show up in these names early and clearly. If SPY opens strongly green, these are often among the first places to look for setups already lining up with the day's direction. The caveat: any individual stock can still break from the index on its own news — being a "market leader" doesn't make a stock immune to its own story.

Oil — A Genuinely Different Animal

Oil prices are driven primarily by global supply and demand for oil itself, not by the same things that move SPY day to day. Oil and the broader market can move together, move opposite, or move independently, depending on why oil is moving: a supply disruption pushing oil up is often read as a cost pressure on the economy (oil up, SPY down); oil rising because global demand is strong can coincide with stocks rising too (oil up, SPY up). "Oil is down" isn't a signal with one fixed meaning — it's context that means different things depending on the story behind it.

The Honest Caveat

All of the above are tendencies based on how these instruments are built — index composition, sector weightings — not laws. They can and do break down, especially around unusual events, which is exactly why this is something to keep observing rather than a fixed rulebook to memorize once.

So, Research and Rumors Say a Crash Is Coming. Now What?

This is the question this section exists to answer — and the honest answer is mostly about what not to do.

  1. Check which one it actually is. Is this a specific data point (an inversion, rising jobless claims) or a vibe (headlines, social posts, "everyone's saying")? Research with a long, variable lead time is not the same as a countdown.
  2. Remember the VIX section. If it's reached the point where it's widely discussed, options-based exposure — the method in Section 01 — is probably already more expensive than it was. Rushing to buy puts now, in response to the rumor, is the exact timing trap that section warned about.
  3. The useful actions are usually boring. Review your Defense Toolkit allocations (Section 04) — is your cash position where you want it? Is your portfolio still diversified the way you intended? These don't require split-second timing.
  4. If you already have a plan, you don't need a new one. If you've set up the kind of ongoing, small "insurance" allocation described in Section 01, "the market might crash" doesn't require you to do anything different — that's the entire point of having decided this in advance. Section 06 (Your Plan) is where this gets written down concretely.
Briefing 04
Defense Toolkit
Low Risk — Foundational Doesn't depend on any single trade going right
Section 01's method works best as a small, deliberate slice of a portfolio — not the whole thing. This section covers the rest: the parts of a plan that don't need any single trade to work out.

Your First Line of Defense: Cash

An emergency fund — typically 3 to 6 months of essential expenses, held in cash or cash-equivalents, separate from anything invested — is the foundation everything else sits on. It's not exciting, and it's not what this guide leads with, but it's what makes everything else optional rather than forced.

During a downturn, cash does double duty: it means you're not forced to sell investments at a loss to cover bills, and it's the source of capital for Section 01's method or for the "buying the dip" idea later in this section. Without it, both of those become reactions to a crisis instead of decisions made on a calm day.

Diversification That Actually Works

"I own 15 different stocks" isn't necessarily diversified if those 15 stocks all move together. Section 03 covered this directly: SPY, VOO, IVV, QQQ, and large market-leader stocks tend to move in the same direction on most days — owning several of them isn't diversification, it's the same bet sized up.

Real diversification means owning things that respond differently to the same event — different asset classes (stocks, bonds, cash, commodities), not just different tickers within the same asset class. The test isn't "do I own different things," it's "if one of these drops sharply, what happens to the others?"

Defensive Assets: Bonds, Gold & Cash Equivalents

Bonds — particularly government bonds — have historically often risen in price when stocks fall sharply, as investors move toward perceived safety (this is part of why the yield curve material in Section 03 matters: bond prices and yields move inversely). Gold is a traditional "safe haven" asset with a long history of holding or gaining value during periods of stock market stress — though it's volatile in its own right and can fall too. Treasury bills and other cash equivalents are the lowest-risk, most liquid option — lowest potential return, but the most predictable.

The Honest Caveat

"Stocks down, bonds up" is a tendency, not a law. 2022 was a notable exception — a year where both stocks and bonds fell together, because the cause (rapidly rising interest rates) hurt both at the same time. Defensive assets reduce risk; they don't eliminate it, and they don't always move the way the textbook description suggests.

Rebalancing — The Quiet Strategy

Rebalancing means periodically adjusting your portfolio back to a target mix — say, 70% stocks / 30% bonds — by selling a bit of whatever has grown and buying a bit of whatever has shrunk. The effect is that it automatically forces a version of "sell high, buy low" without requiring you to predict anything. It's unglamorous, and that's the point — it's one of the few strategies that works because it doesn't depend on being right about the future.

Buying the Dip, Properly

"Buy the dip" is good advice that gets used badly. Done properly, it means continuing a regular, planned investment schedule (sometimes called dollar-cost averaging) through a decline rather than pausing it — buying more shares for the same dollar amount when prices are lower, as part of a plan you already had.

Here's why pausing during a decline is so costly: across decades of market history, roughly 78% of the S&P 500's best individual trading days have occurred either during bear markets or within the first two months of a new bull market. In practice, that means the investor who steps away "to wait for things to settle down" often misses the sharpest part of the recovery — in the 2020 example from Section 02, the rebound included a single day where the index gained over 9% — entirely.

Done improperly, "buying the dip" means trying to guess the exact bottom and putting in a large lump sum at what you hope is the lowest point — which is a forecasting problem, not a plan. The properly-done version doesn't require knowing where the bottom is at all.

Briefing 05
Psychology
Applies to Everything in This Guide The most common reason any of the above stops working
Every section so far has been about mechanics — options, history, signals, defense. This one is about why people who understand all of that still lose money. The mechanics are rarely the problem. The reaction to seeing them play out in real time usually is.

The Crash Psychology Cycle

Crashes tend to move through a recognizable emotional sequence: first denial ("this is just a normal dip"), then concern, then fear as the decline continues and headlines escalate, then — often near the actual bottom — outright panic and capitulation, where people sell simply because they can't tolerate watching the number drop anymore. The cruel part: capitulation tends to cluster right before the sharpest recoveries, which is exactly the 78% statistic from Section 04. The emotional low point and the market's low point are often nearly the same moment — which means the decision made at peak panic is frequently the worst-timed decision available.

The Specific Trap This Guide Has Warned About Twice

Section 01 and Section 03 both made the same point from different angles: by the time something is widely discussed as "the market is crashing," a lot of the pricing has already adjusted — implied volatility has already risen, options are already more expensive. The psychological pull to act at exactly that moment — when the fear is most widely shared and most validated by headlines — is also the moment when the specific method in Section 01 is least favorably priced. The plan in Section 06 exists specifically to separate "I have a plan for this" from "I am reacting to this right now."

What NOT to Do

  • Don't check your account compulsively during high volatility. More observations of a volatile number doesn't produce better decisions — it produces more opportunities to react emotionally to noise.
  • Don't abandon your plan mid-decline. A plan made on a calm day, for the express purpose of handling a volatile one, loses its entire value if it gets discarded the moment it's actually needed.
  • Don't add to a losing position out of frustration. "Doubling down to make it back faster" turns a defined, planned-for loss (Section 01's dollar math) into an undefined one.
  • Don't chase the move after it's already happened. Buying deep OTM puts after a sharp drop, out of fear of "missing it" or anger about not having bought earlier, is buying into the exact high-IV, expensive conditions Section 01 warned about.
  • Don't let leverage turn a survivable loss into an account-ending one. Every dollar figure in this guide assumed money you'd decided in advance you could lose. Borrowed money, or money pulled from the emergency fund in Section 04, breaks that assumption.

Your Plan

Everything in this guide is meant to be decided now, on a calm day — not improvised later. This page is the result: a checklist that ties Sections 01, 04, and 05 together. Work through it, check off what you've actually decided, and come back to it before you act on anything in Section 01.
The Method — Section 01
Tickers: I've identified 1–3 optionable stocks or ETFs I actually understand and watch regularly.

Psychology — Section 05
Check-in limit: I've set a maximum number of times per day I'll check my accounts during high volatility.

Every other section in this guide is information. This one is a decision — made now, while nothing is on fire, so that when something eventually is, you're executing a plan instead of inventing one.

Put OptionA contract giving the right to sell a stock at a set price (the strike) before a set date (expiration).

Strike PriceThe price at which an option lets you buy (call) or sell (put) the underlying stock.
Expiration DateThe date after which the option no longer exists. If it hasn't paid off by then, it's worth nothing.
Out of the Money (OTM)A put with a strike below the current price (or a call with a strike above it) — only valuable if the stock moves to reach it.
PremiumThe price of an option, quoted per share. Multiply by 100 for the actual per-contract cost.
ContractOne options contract represents 100 shares of the underlying stock.
Implied Volatility (IV)The market's expectation of how much a stock will move, baked into option prices. Higher IV = more expensive options.
ThetaThe rate at which an option loses value purely from time passing — accelerates as expiration approaches.
GammaHow much an option's sensitivity to price changes itself — higher gamma means bigger swings in the option's value for the same stock move.
StrangleBuying an OTM put and an OTM call on the same stock at the same time — profits from a big move in either direction.
VIXAn index measuring expected S&P 500 volatility over the next 30 days, based on options prices. Often called the "fear gauge."
Yield Curve / InversionThe relationship between short- and long-term bond interest rates. An "inversion" — short-term rates higher than long-term — has preceded every US recession since 1971, with a long and variable lead time.
DiversificationOwning assets that respond differently to the same event — not just owning many things that move together.
RebalancingPeriodically adjusting a portfolio back to target percentages by trimming what's grown and adding to what's shrunk.
Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)Investing a fixed amount on a regular schedule, regardless of price — buys more shares when prices are low, fewer when high.
Emergency FundCash set aside (typically 3–6 months of expenses) separate from investments, for unplanned needs.
Treasury Bills (T-Bills)Short-term US government debt — among the lowest-risk, most liquid cash-equivalent investments.

Bear MarketA decline of 20% or more from a recent high.
Bid/Ask SpreadThe gap between the highest price a buyer will pay (bid) and the lowest a seller will accept (ask). Wider on less-liquid options.
